# The Olive Tree of Al-Najma

---

## Part One: The Last Harvest

The sun rose over Gaza like a wound opening. Yusuf al-Saadi stood at the edge of his family's olive grove, his weathered hands resting on the gnarled trunk of the oldest tree—the one his great-grandfather had planted a century ago. The tree had witnessed everything. Ottoman soldiers marching through. British mandates. Wars. Refugees. The birth of his children and the death of his wife. It had stood firm, its roots deep in the limestone soil, its branches reaching toward a sky that had never quite been free.

But today, the sky was different. Today, the sky was full of drones.

Yusuf's granddaughter, Layla, ran to him, her small feet kicking up dust. She was twelve years old, with eyes that held too much sadness for someone so young. "Jiddo," she called, using the Arabic word for grandfather. "The soldiers are at the checkpoint. They say we have to leave. Again."

Yusuf closed his eyes. He had heard those words many times. First in 1948, when his grandmother had fled Jaffa with nothing but a key and a handful of soil. Then in 1967. Then in 2008, 2014, and 2021—each war taking more than the last. His family had been uprooted so many times that they had roots in a dozen different graves.

"Come," he said gently, taking Layla's hand. "Help me with the harvest. There is still time."

They worked together, the old man and the girl, plucking the green and black olives from the branches. The fruit was bitter this year—the soil had been poisoned by Israeli settler attacks, the trees scarred by bulldozers. But Yusuf knew how to coax life from dead land. He always had.

"Why do we stay, Jiddo?" Layla asked, her voice trembling. "Everyone else has left. My friends are all gone. Their houses are rubble. Why do we stay?"

Yusuf paused, holding an olive in his palm. It was small and imperfect, but it was alive.

"Because this is where we belong," he said. "This land remembers our footsteps. It remembers the songs of our ancestors. If we leave, who will remember?"

---

## Part Two: The Occupation

The occupation had a face, but it was always changing. Sometimes it was a soldier with a gun, shouting orders in a language Layla couldn't understand. Sometimes it was a bulldozer, tearing through homes and orchards as if they were made of paper. Sometimes it was a settlement, creeping across the hills like a disease, swallowing villages whole.

But the worst face was the most ordinary: the settlers who came in the night and burned the olive trees. They would pour kerosene at the roots and light a match, watching the flames devour centuries of history. Yusuf had seen it happen a dozen times. Each time, he would stand among the ashes and weep.

"Jiddo," Layla asked one evening, as they sat on the roof of their stone house, watching the orange glow of a settlement on the distant hill, "why do they hate us so much?"

Yusuf sighed. "They don't hate us, habibti. They fear us. Because we are still here. Because we will always be here. That is the greatest threat to them—not our weapons, but our presence."

"But how can they build on our land and say it's theirs?"

"They rewrite history," Yusuf said bitterly. "They have powerful friends, powerful media. They tell the world that we never existed. That this land was empty. But I have the deeds to this land. I have the keys to my grandmother's house in Jaffa. I have the memory of my father's voice. They can destroy our homes, but they cannot destroy our memory."

---

## Part Three: The Siege

The siege came in waves. It was not a single event but a slow strangling—a tightening of the noose around Gaza's neck. The Israeli government called it "security measures." The world called it a blockade. Yusuf called it what it was: a cage.

Food was scarce. Medicine was scarce. Hope was scarce. Every day, Layla watched the children in her neighborhood grow thinner, paler, more hollow-eyed. The schools had been bombed. The hospital was overwhelmed. The water was undrinkable.

One night, a drone strike hit the building next door. The explosion threw Layla from her bed. She woke up in the rubble, her ears ringing, her face covered in dust and blood.

"Jiddo!" she screamed. "Jiddo!"

She found him pinned under a fallen beam. His leg was broken, his face was pale, but his eyes were still alive. He reached out and touched her cheek.

"Did you think I would leave you?" he whispered.

The ambulance took three hours to arrive. By then, Yusuf had lost too much blood. In the hospital—a cramped, underfunded facility that was running out of supplies—the doctors did what they could, but they were overwhelmed. The siege had stripped them of everything: anesthetics, antibiotics, hope.

"Jiddo," Layla whispered, holding his hand in the dark hospital ward, "don't leave me."

Yusuf smiled weakly. "I am not leaving you, habibti. I am going to join the ancestors. The olive trees. The soil. I am going back to the land."

He pressed something into her palm: an old brass key, tarnished and worn.

"This is the key to my grandmother's house in Jaffa," he said. "One day, you will go there. You will open that door. You will walk through it, and you will not be afraid. Promise me."

"I promise," Layla wept.

Yusuf closed his eyes for the last time.

---

## Part Four: The Key

The siege continued. The bombs fell. The children died. The world watched. But Layla held on to the key. She held on to it like a prayer, like a promise, like a seed buried in the cold earth, waiting for the spring.

She grew older. She grew harder. She became a voice—a voice that spoke for the silenced, the buried, the forgotten. She wrote poems about her grandfather's olive grove. She gave speeches at rallies. She carried the key around her neck, a talisman of resistance, a memory of home.

"To the world," she said at a United Nations conference, addressing the diplomats in their suits and ties, "this is a key to a house that no longer exists. To me, it is proof that my grandmother once walked these streets. That my grandfather planted these trees. That we are not a nation of ghosts. We are a people. We are here. And we will not vanish."

The applause was polite. The diplomats made their speeches. The resolutions were passed. And then the bombs fell again.

---

## Part Five: The Seed

Layla returned to Gaza, to the ruins of her grandfather's house. The olive grove was gone—bulldozed, burned, salted with hatred. But she found something among the ashes: a single seed, black and hard, the last remnant of the oldest tree.

She held it in her palm and wept. It was so small. So fragile. But it was still alive.

She planted the seed in a pot, in the rubble of the house. She watered it with her tears. She whispered to it in Arabic, the language of her ancestors.

"Grow," she said. "Grow for Jiddo. Grow for the children who never got to see the sun. Grow because we are still here."

Weeks passed. The siege continued. The world remained silent. But the seed sprouted. A tiny green shoot, fragile and defiant, pushed through the soil.

Layla smiled. It was a small victory, but it was enough.

---

## Part Six: The Promise

Years later, Layla held a small seedling in her hands. She was older now, her hair streaked with gray, her face etched with grief and determination. But her eyes still burned with the same fire she had as a child.

She traveled to Jaffa, to the house of her grandmother. It was now occupied by an Israeli family. The new owners had renovated it, painted it, changed its name. But Layla knew—she would always know—what it truly was.

She did not demand the house back. She did not argue with the settlers. Instead, she went to the garden and planted the seedling. It was a small olive tree, a descendant of her grandfather's orchard.

The settlers watched her suspiciously. "What are you doing?" they demanded.

Layla looked up at them, her face calm, her voice steady. "I am planting a tree. My grandfather planted trees here a hundred years ago. He was displaced. His home was taken. But the land remembers. The tree will grow. And one day, my grandchildren will come and sit under its shade, and they will know that this is their land, that they belong here, that no amount of hatred can erase their roots."

The settlers did not understand. They saw only a dangerous woman with a defiant message. But Layla didn't care. She had done what her grandfather asked.

She had remembered. She had planted. She had promised.

---

## Epilogue: The Tree

Today, in the city of Jaffa, there is an olive tree that grows in a garden that was once a Palestinian home. It is not a large tree. It is not particularly old. But it is alive.

On its trunk, someone has carved the Arabic word: *"Sumud"*—steadfastness, resilience, the refusal to be erased.

At night, when the wind blows through its branches, it whispers the names of those who are gone. Yusuf. His grandmother. All the children who never grew up. All the olives that were never harvested. All the homes that were never rebuilt.

But the tree still stands. And so does the hope.

Because the land remembers. Because the people remember. Because the seed of justice—small and fragile and buried beneath the weight of oppression—will never stop trying to break through the soil.

**And that, in the end, is what Palestine is.**

Not just a place. Not just a conflict. Not just a headline.

**It is a tree. It is a key. It is a promise kept through generations of struggle, grief, and unbreakable hope.**
